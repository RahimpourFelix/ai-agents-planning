## Case one: internal usage

Intelligence plus electricity creates a Knowken.
Once that Knowken exists, every additional call becomes cheaper.

You don’t need to “rethink” the problem again and again.

That means less token usage, lower electricity consumption, and faster responses.

While the first call might be more expesnive, all following calls are cheaper.

## Case two: business to customer

A company like Brückner could build Knowkens and expose them to their customers.

Instead of handling repeated support requests, the Knowken carries that intelligence.
Customers get answers immediately. Support load drops.

Again, fewer tokens, less electricity, faster outcomes.

Its efficient, lower cost support where its needed, calculated on the most recent data.

## Case three: business to business

A company that gathers large amounts of data doesn’t have to sell the raw data anymore.
It can sell the intelligence derived from it.

Example Agora: Instead of distributing full datasets about electricity usage in Germany, 
they could provide Knowkens that answer specific questions for high-consumption customers (like aluminium manufacturers).

No api or database access is necessary. 
Data doesn't need to be interpreted, it is provided with a kind of MicroAI (most likely a gradient boost model).

“Data as a product” is moved to "Knowledge as a service".
One would subscribe to intelligence, not to data access.
